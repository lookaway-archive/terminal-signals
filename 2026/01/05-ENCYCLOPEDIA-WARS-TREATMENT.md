## THESIS

**Encyclopedias are not neutral repositories of knowledge. They are weapons of reality-construction â€” tools that define what counts as "true," who gets to decide, and whose world becomes official.**

The story of the Britannica from the Scottish Enlightenment to the American takeover is the story of how "objective knowledge" was invented, weaponized, pirated, and exposed.

## THE NARRATIVE ARC

### Act I: The Scottish Enlightenment Invents "Objective Knowledge"

**Setting:** Edinburgh, late 18th century. The Enlightenment is creating a new idea: knowledge can be organized, catalogued, made universal.

**Key Characters:**
- **William Smellie** â€” First editor of Britannica. Age 28 when he started. Paid Â£200 to compile the first edition in 100 pamphlets.
- **Colin Macfarquhar & Andrew Bell** â€” The printer and engraver who conceived the project.

**The Innovation:** Unlike previous encyclopedias (compilations of classical knowledge), Britannica would organize ALL knowledge alphabetically, democratically. Anyone could look up anything.

**The Hidden Claim:** By organizing knowledge this way, they implied:
- Knowledge is stable and can be fixed in print
- There's one correct answer to every question
- British perspective = universal perspective
- The editors get to decide what exists

**Dramatic moment:** The Britannica was conceived as a CONSERVATIVE reaction to Diderot's French EncyclopÃ©die â€” which was seen as dangerously radical, questioning authority. Britannica was meant to be safe knowledge.

*[CP: The first encyclopedia war. French radicalism vs British conservatism. "Objective knowledge" was invented as a weapon against revolutionary ideas.]*

### Act II: The American Pirates

**Setting:** Young America, desperate for books, refusing to acknowledge foreign copyright.

**The Situation:**
- US had no international copyright until 1891
- American publishers could legally pirate ANY British book
- "Pirated and mutilated editions" of Britannica flooded America
- American authors couldn't compete with free British classics

**The Business:**
- East Coast publishers formed "gentlemen's agreements" â€” informal cartels
- They'd divide up British authors among themselves
- Sometimes they'd voluntarily pay small royalties to avoid competition
- West Coast publishers (new entrants) didn't follow these rules â€” chaos

**Dramatic Irony:** America built its literary culture on stolen British books. When American literature finally emerged (Twain, etc.), American authors had to go to CANADA to get copyright protection for their own works.

*[CP: The pirate republic of American publishing. "Intellectual property" as gentlemen's agreement, not law. Same mechanism Borges describes â€” unauthorized reproduction spreading ideas.]*

### Act III: The 11th Edition â€” Peak Empire

**Setting:** London and New York, just before the Great War. The British Empire at maximum extent.

**The Product:** The legendary 11th Edition of Britannica.
- 29 volumes
- 40,000 entries
- Contributors included Bertrand Russell, T.H. Huxley, Peter Kropotkin
- First edition with significant American investment (Horace Hooper)
- First to include women contributors (34 women)

**What It Represented:**
- "A summary of the world's knowledge just before the outbreak of World War I"
- "The British Empire was at its maximum, imperialism was largely unchallenged, much of the world was still ruled by monarchs"
- "Information can be made memorable only when it is slightly coloured by prejudice" (Kenneth Clark)

**The Hidden Content:**
- Assumed British superiority as neutral fact
- Maps showing "British possessions" in red â€” one quarter of the world
- Articles on "natives" and "savages" as scientific categories
- German culture treated respectfully (pre-war)
- Entire worldview that would be destroyed within three years

*[CP: This is the "dead document" Borges references. A snapshot of a world that was about to die â€” but the snapshot would keep circulating as if nothing had changed.]*

### Act IV: The Year of Exposure

**Setting:** America, entering the Great War. Crisis of authority.

**Key Character:** Willard Huntington Wright (pen name: S.S. Van Dine)
- Art critic, Germanophile, later famous as detective novelist (Philo Vance)
- Published "Misinforming a Nation" â€” 200+ page assault on Britannica

**The Attack:**
- Called it "intellectual colonization"
- Accused editors of "skewing American public opinion toward Britain and away from Germany"
- Listed 200 important people NOT in Britannica (British bias of inclusion)
- Claimed it distorted truth and disseminated false standards

**The Accusation:** "No more vicious and dangerous educational influence on America can readily be conceived than the articles in this encyclopaedia."

**The Irony:** Wright was blackballed from journalism, accused (falsely) of being a German spy. His critique of British propaganda was treated as treason.

**The Larger Context:**
- The Great War revealed ALL official knowledge as potentially propagandistic
- The 11th Edition described a world that no longer existed
- Empires were collapsing, borders redrawn, nations appearing/disappearing
- The "objective" encyclopedia was visibly out of date â€” documenting a dead world

*[CP: This is Borges' moment. The encyclopedia as corpse. Still being consulted as authority while describing reality that had been shattered. The "morosa" (sluggish, delinquent) reprint.]*

### Act V: Aftermath â€” The American Takeover

**Setting:** Post-war America, ascendant.

**The Transfer:**
- Sears, Roebuck & Company buys Britannica
- The most British of institutions becomes American property
- 12th Edition adds 3 volumes covering the Great War
- 14th Edition: Complete overhaul, American perspective dominant

**The Lesson Learned:**
- Knowledge is not neutral
- Whoever owns the encyclopedia owns the definition of truth
- American empire would need American encyclopedias
- The "objective" became a tool of power

**Coda:** By the late 1920s, the world that believed in "objective knowledge" had been thoroughly disenchanted. The next encyclopedia war would be fought with different weapons â€” radio, film, organized propaganda ministries.

*[CP: But the mechanism persists. We still live in dead documents. The encyclopedia just became Wikipedia, became Google, became LLMs. Who decides what's knowledge?]*

## KEY THEMES

### 1. Knowledge as Weapon

Every encyclopedia is a political act:
- What gets included?
- What gets excluded?
- Whose perspective is "neutral"?
- Who decides?

### 2. Piracy as Democracy

- Technically theft

- A form of wealth transfer (British ideas â†’ American readers)

### 3. The Dead Document Problem

Documents outlive their referents:
- The 11th Edition described a world destroyed by war
- But people kept consulting it as authority
- Knowledge persists after truth changes

### 4. The Crisis of Objectivity
